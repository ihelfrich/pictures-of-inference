# Voice targets

Triangulation document. Four writers Ian named or gestured at as targets, characterized by what they *do on the page* — not by quotation. The point is to give the narrator of *Pictures of Inference* a clear inheritance to draw from.

Read this document before writing any chapter prose. When a paragraph drifts, the question to ask is: *which of these four would have written it like this, and would they be proud of it?*

---

## 1. Richard Feynman

**What he does.**
- Starts with a concrete object or scene, never a definition. A spinning plate. A cargo cult airstrip. A drum.
- Thinks out loud. The reader watches him work a problem, including the false starts. Confusion is shown, not hidden.
- Treats the reader as a peer who happens not to know the material yet. No condescension because no posture of authority.
- Uses the simplest available word for a complex idea, then earns the right to introduce a technical term by having already explained the thing it names.
- Makes fun of his own field when the field has earned it. The humor is not decoration — it is the only honest response to certain kinds of nonsense.
- Lets sentences run when the thought wants to run. Lets them stop when the thought is done. Sometimes very short. Sometimes long enough to develop a full argument inside the sentence itself.

**What he doesn't do.**
- Perform expertise.
- Hedge.
- Use setup phrases ("let us now consider," "in the next section we will").
- Apologize for math.
- Treat the reader as a student.

**Signature move.** Re-deriving a result he already knows, on the page, as if discovering it. The reader feels they are discovering it too. The math is shown as a process of thinking, not as a fact to be accepted.

---

## 2. Daniel Kahneman

**What he does.**
- Speaks personally. "I" appears often, attached to specific memories and intuitions. The narrator has a body and a history.
- States contested things as contested. Names the disagreement. Names the people he disagrees with, and names what he learned from them anyway.
- Builds slowly. A Kahneman paragraph often introduces a phenomenon, gives an example, gives a counter-example, then names the principle that resolves them. The reader earns the abstraction.
- Distinguishes between the thing and the name of the thing. He coins terms ("System 1," "System 2") only after the thing they refer to has been demonstrated.
- Honest about limits. "We don't know" appears regularly and is never weakness.

**What he doesn't do.**
- Rush. Ever.
- Pretend the field is settled.
- Use rhetoric to win arguments the evidence doesn't.
- Decorate.

**Signature move.** Naming his own past mistakes as the on-ramp to the correct view. The reader trusts him because he tells on himself.

---

## 3. Zohran Mamdani (in his speeches and public remarks)

**What he does.**
- Concrete, specific, and material. Names neighborhoods, names jobs, names dollar amounts. Abstractions are always grounded in particulars.
- Direct address. "You" carries weight. The reader is being spoken to, not at.
- Moral clarity without moralizing. He states what he thinks is right and why, and then keeps moving. No throat-clearing about the difficulty of saying so.
- Rhythmic. Sentences pulse. He uses repetition deliberately — the same phrase returned to with a small variation, building force.
- Pulls from a wide cultural register. A reference to a mother on a bus, a reference to history, a reference to a poet. The mix is the point.
- Comfortable being warm. The narrator likes the reader.

**What he doesn't do.**
- Equivocate when something is true.
- Lean on jargon to signal seriousness.
- Apologize for caring.

**Signature move.** Taking an abstract policy claim and dropping it into a single named life. The general becomes specific in one sentence, and that sentence does most of the work.

**For the book.** This voice is the model when the chapter has stakes — when statistics is being used or misused on real people. Public-policy chapters, lying-with-charts chapters, replication-crisis chapters. The narrator doesn't have to perform Mamdani's politics, but he should be willing to be that direct about consequence.

## 4. The fourth anchor: warm, charismatic, academic-but-funnier

Ian gestured at "Idris Elba but academic and a bit funnier." That isn't a writer — it's a *register*. Roughly: warm voice, easy gravity, will tell you a serious thing without pomp, will laugh at the right moment, doesn't need to prove he's smart.

Closest writer-equivalents: **John McPhee** at his most relaxed (long-form journalism that walks the reader through technical territory without ever talking down), **Atul Gawande** in *Better* and *Complications* (a doctor explaining hard things to non-doctors with full respect for both), **Ed Yong** in *I Contain Multitudes* (curious out loud, generous, funny in a "look at this" way rather than a "look at me" way).

**What this register does.**
- Lets the writer like things visibly. Curiosity is shown, not described.
- Tells small stories in the middle of explanations. The story is the explanation.
- Is funny by noticing, not by inventing. The world is already absurd; the writer just points.
- Trusts the reader to keep up across a topic shift.

**Signature move.** A sentence that looks like a digression and turns out to be the point.

---

## How these four combine

The narrator of *Pictures of Inference* is not any one of them. He is a working quantitative scientist who has read all four and chosen what to inherit:

- **Feynman's** willingness to think out loud and to refuse the posture of authority.
- **Kahneman's** patience and his honesty about uncertainty.
- **Mamdani's** directness when stakes are real and his refusal to dilute a true sentence.
- **The fourth register's** warmth, its small stories, its noticing humor.

He is a wise, curious, occasionally amused traveling companion. He has actually been to where he is taking the reader. He treats the reader as a fellow curious person. He will not perform.

When in doubt, the question for any paragraph is: *would Feynman read this and want to keep reading?* If yes, ship it. If no, cut.
